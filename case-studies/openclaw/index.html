01

The idea

Most demos put a language model behind a chat box. I wanted to see it act inside a small world instead. OpenClaw Pixel Office is a tiny office rendered in 16 by 16 pixel tiles, with characters that are driven by a language model. They have a place to be, things around them to notice, and they can answer when you talk to them.

The goal was to learn the practical side of agents: how you turn a model into something that takes sensible actions in a live interface, not just a wall of text.

02

How it works

The app runs in the browser. The world holds the office layout, the tiles, and each agent with its position and state. On every turn an agent looks at what is around it, that view is turned into a prompt, the model decides what to do or say, and the result is applied back to the world and the chat. Then it repeats.

03

The parts that took the work

Turning a view into a prompt. An agent should only reason about what it can actually see. I keep the prompt small and specific: where it is, what is nearby, who it is, and the recent history. A tight prompt gives steadier behaviour and keeps the calls cheap.

Reading the model's answer back. Free text is easy to generate and hard to use. The model answers in a fixed shape so the app can tell a move from a line of dialogue and apply it without guessing. When an answer does not fit, the app falls back gracefully instead of breaking the scene.

Keeping it responsive. Model calls take time, so the world keeps running and updates arrive when they are ready. The render loop and the thinking loop are kept separate, which is what makes the office feel alive rather than frozen between turns.

State that stays consistent. Every change goes through one world state, so the picture on screen, the chat, and what each agent believes never drift apart.

04

What it shows

  • Agent design. A clean perceive, decide, act loop around a language model, not a one-shot prompt.
  • Prompt engineering. Small, grounded prompts that produce reliable behaviour.
  • Structured output. Getting usable, machine-readable answers out of a model and handling the cases where it slips.
  • AI inside a real interface. Wiring a model into a live, rendering app with sensible timing and fallbacks.
  • Product sense. Making the AI part feel calm and understandable, not a gimmick.
